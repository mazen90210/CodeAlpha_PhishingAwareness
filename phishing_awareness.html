What is phishing?

⚠️ Definition

The core concept

Phishing is a cyberattack where criminals impersonate trusted entities — banks, companies, colleagues, or government agencies — to trick you into revealing sensitive information or installing malware.

🔄 How an attack unfolds

Step-by-step attack flow

  • Step 1: Attacker researches the target and crafts a convincing message
  • Step 2: Victim receives a fake email, SMS, or call from a trusted-looking source
  • Step 3: Victim clicks a malicious link or downloads an infected attachment
  • Step 4: Victim is sent to a fake website or malware is silently installed
  • Step 5: Attacker steals credentials, data, or money
Real example

PayPal phishing (2023)

Millions received emails claiming "Your account is limited." A fake PayPal site stole login credentials from thousands of victims worldwide.

Real example

Google Docs scam (2017)

A worm spread via fake Google Docs sharing invites. It tricked users into granting access to their entire Gmail account and contact list.

Spotting a phishing email

From:security@paypa1-support.com
To:you@email.com
Subject:URGENT: Your account will be suspended in 24 hours!
Dear Valued Customer,

We have detected suspicious activity on your account. Your access will be SUSPENDED unless you verify your information immediately.

Verify my account now → http://paypa1-verify.xyz/login

This is urgent. Failure to act will result in permanent account closure. Do not ignore this message.

Regards,
PayPal Security Team

Red flags detected:

❌ Fake domain: "paypa1-support.com" — notice the number 1 instead of letter l
❌ False urgency: "24 hours", "URGENT", "immediately", "do not ignore"
❌ Threats: "permanent account closure" to pressure you into acting fast
❌ Suspicious link: "paypa1-verify.xyz" — not the real paypal.com
❌ Generic greeting: "Dear Valued Customer" — real companies use your name
❌ Excessive formatting: bold words meant to trigger panic

Types of phishing attacks

📧
Most common

Email phishing

Mass emails sent to thousands impersonating banks, services, or companies. Relies on volume — even a small success rate yields results.

🎯
Targeted

Spear phishing

Highly personalized attack using researched details (name, job, colleagues). Much harder to detect — looks completely legitimate.

📱
SMS-based

Smishing

Phishing via text messages. "Your package is delayed. Track it: [fake link]" — exploits mobile users who click without checking.

📞
Voice-based

Vishing

Phone calls from fake bank staff, tech support, or government officials asking you to reveal account details verbally.

👑
High-value targets

Whaling

Spear phishing targeting executives (CEOs, CFOs). Attackers study the target for weeks to craft highly convincing messages.

🌐
Website-based

Pharming

Redirects users from a real website to a fake one without any clicking. Even typing the correct URL can lead to the attacker's site.

🧠

Social engineering tactics

Phishing works by exploiting human psychology, not just technology. Here are the main manipulation techniques attackers use:

⏰

Urgency and scarcity

Creating time pressure ("Act within 24 hours!") forces victims to skip their normal caution and react emotionally rather than rationally.

👮

Authority impersonation

Pretending to be a boss, IT department, bank, or government agency. People are conditioned to comply with authority figures without questioning.

🤝

Trust and familiarity

Using the victim's name, job title, or references to real colleagues makes the attack feel personal and legitimate, lowering suspicion.

⚠️

Fear and threats

"Your account will be permanently closed" or "Legal action will be taken" — threats of consequences trigger panic and override rational thinking.

🎁

Greed and rewards

"You've won a prize!" or "Claim your refund now" — the promise of something valuable lowers a victim's guard and motivates quick action.

🔒

How to protect yourself

Before you click anything

  • ✅Check the sender's email address carefully — look for misspellings like "paypa1" or extra characters
  • ✅Hover over links to see the real URL before clicking — the displayed text and actual URL can be completely different
  • ✅Slow down when a message creates urgency — that pressure is the attack working on you
  • ✅Never open attachments from unknown senders — even PDFs and Word docs can contain malware

Secure your accounts

  • ✅Enable two-factor authentication (2FA) on all important accounts — even if your password is stolen, the attacker can't log in
  • ✅Use a password manager and unique passwords for every account — a breach on one site won't affect others
  • ✅Keep your browser, OS, and antivirus software updated — patches fix vulnerabilities attackers exploit
🚨

Never enter passwords on a site you reached by clicking a link in an email. Always navigate directly to the website yourself.

🚨

If you receive a suspicious email from your bank, call them directly using the number on the back of your card — never use the number in the email.

💡

If you think you've been phished: change your passwords immediately, enable 2FA, and report it to your IT department or the impersonated company.
